Pillar 1

How AWS charges you

It's a utility model — like electricity. You don't buy the power plant; you pay for what you draw, and you get better rates if you commit.

Three pricing principles (AWS's official phrasing):

  • Pay as you go — pay only for what you use, no big upfront cost, no long contracts required.
  • Save when you commit — Reserved Instances / Savings Plans give big discounts for a 1–3 year commitment.
  • Pay less as AWS grows — economies of scale + tiered/volume discounts mean the more you use, the lower the per-unit price.

Three things you actually pay for (the cost drivers): Compute, Storage, and Data transfer.

The data-transfer gotcha (tested)

Data IN to AWS (uploads) is generally free. Data OUT to the internet costs money. Transfer within the same Region is usually free or cheap. So "what's expensive?" → moving data out.

Pillar 2 · the heart of Domain 4

The four cost tools (don't mix them up)

This is where most points are won or lost — four tools that sound similar. The clean way to keep them straight is when in time each one operates:

ToolWhenIts job
Pricing CalculatorBeforeEstimate the cost of an architecture before you build it. Planning a budget for a new project.
AWS BudgetsWatchingSet custom spend/usage limits and get alerts when you cross (or are forecast to cross) them. Proactive.
Cost ExplorerAfterVisualize and analyze your past & current spend over time, spot trends, forecast. "Where did the money go?"
Cost & Usage Report (CUR)RawThe most detailed billing data — every line item, hourly — exported to S3 for deep analysis.
The one-line analogy

Pricing Calculator = the quote before the job. Budgets = the smoke alarm. Cost Explorer = reviewing your bank statement. CUR = the itemized receipt with every line.

Cost allocation tags — tag resources (e.g. team:marketing) so Cost Explorer/CUR can break spend down by team, project, or environment.

04
Pillar 3

Many accounts → one bill

AWS Organizations lets a company centrally manage many AWS accounts. Its billing superpower is Consolidated Billing:

  • One bill for every account in the organization (one payer account).
  • Combined usage = bigger volume discounts — all accounts' usage is pooled, so you hit cheaper pricing tiers sooner, shared across accounts.
  • Easier tracking, plus central governance (SCPs — more of a security topic).
Trigger

"One bill for multiple accounts" / "share volume discounts across accounts" → AWS Organizations + Consolidated Billing.

Pillar 4 · the other big chunk

The support-plan ladder

Five tiers, cheapest to priciest. You don't memorize exact dollars — you learn what each tier unlocks, because that's how questions are phrased.

PlanRough priceWhat it unlocks (the tested bits)
BasicFreeDocs, whitepapers, forums, 24/7 customer service for account/billing, core Trusted Advisor checks, Health Dashboard. No technical support.
Developer~$29/moEmail to Cloud Support, business-hours only, 1 contact. For experimenting / dev.
Business~$100/mo24/7 phone, chat & email with engineers, full Trusted Advisor, unlimited contacts, third-party software support. Minimum recommended for production.
Enterprise On-Ramp~$5,500/moA pool of TAMs, <30 min response for business-critical issues, cost-optimization workshops.
Enterprise~$15,000/moDesignated TAM, <15 min response for business-critical, Concierge billing team, Incident Detection & Response, Well-Architected reviews.
Support-plan trigger words

"Just starting / free" → Basic. "24/7 phone support" or "full Trusted Advisor" → Business (the production minimum). "Designated TAM" or "<15 min critical response" → Enterprise ("pool of TAMs" → On-Ramp).

06
Bonus — shows up in billing Qs

AWS Trusted Advisor

An automated advisor that checks your account against best practices in 5 categories:

  • Cost Optimization — find idle / underused resources you're paying for (the billing-relevant one).
  • Security · Fault Tolerance · Performance · Service Limits.

Basic & Developer plans get only the core checks; Business and above unlock all checks. Mnemonic for the 5: "Cost, Security, Fault tolerance, Performance, Service limits."
